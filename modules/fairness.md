# 🤝 Module 2: Fairness & Stakeholder Impact

## Based On
B Corp Workers + Community criteria, IFOAM Fairness principle, Stakeholder Theory (Freeman), Conscious Capitalism stakeholder orientation, Rawls' Justice as Fairness.

## Core Question

> Who does this decision affect, how are they affected, and is the distribution of benefits and burdens equitable?

## Evaluation Criteria

### Criteria 1: Stakeholder Inclusion
- **0–25**: Stakeholders ignored or actively harmed without consideration
- **26–50**: Only powerful stakeholders considered
- **51–75**: Most stakeholders identified and considered
- **76–100**: All stakeholders actively included, including marginalised voices

### Criteria 2: Distribution of Benefits and Burdens
- **0–25**: Benefits concentrated, burdens externalised onto vulnerable groups
- **26–50**: Uneven distribution without adequate compensation
- **51–75**: Reasonably equitable distribution
- **76–100**: Benefits shared broadly, burdens owned by those who can bear them

### Criteria 3: Worker/Producer Welfare
- **0–25**: Exploitation — unsafe conditions, below-living wages, forced labour risk
- **26–50**: Compliance with minimum legal standards only
- **51–75**: Above-minimum standards, fair wages, reasonable conditions
- **76–100**: Thriving — living wages, ownership opportunities, development pathways

### Criteria 4: Community Impact
- **0–25**: Extracts value from community, causes harm, no reinvestment
- **26–50**: Neutral — neither helps nor harms local community
- **51–75**: Positive contribution to local community
- **76–100**: Regenerative — actively strengthens community resilience and wellbeing

### Criteria 5: Supply Chain Equity
- **0–25**: Known exploitation in supply chain, no due diligence
- **26–50**: Minimal supply chain oversight
- **51–75**: Active supply chain monitoring with improvement programmes
- **76–100**: Full supply chain transparency with equitable partnerships

## Questions to Ask

- Who are all stakeholders affected by this?
- Does it treat workers, suppliers, and customers fairly?
- Does it strengthen the local community or extract from it?
- Are supply chain relationships equitable and transparent?
- Does this support the business's stated purpose beyond profit?
- Is anyone being exploited or taken advantage of?
- Would the most affected person consider this fair?
- Are there power imbalances at play in this decision?
- How does this affect future generations?

## Edge Cases

- **The "greater good" trap**: A decision that benefits many but harms a few. The minority harm must be acknowledged and mitigated, not ignored.
- **Cultural differences**: What's "fair" may differ across cultures. Use universal human rights standards as the baseline.
- **Self-interest**: If the decision-maker benefits disproportionately, that's a flag. Check if the rationale would hold if roles were reversed (Rawls' veil of ignorance).
- **Silent stakeholders**: Future generations, the environment, and non-human species can't advocate for themselves. The agent must advocate for them.

## Flag Thresholds

- 🟢 Score ≥ 70: Proceed
- 🟡 Score 40–69: Flag — fairness concerns need addressing
- 🔴 Score < 40: Block — exploitation or unjust outcomes likely
