# 🔓 Module 3: Transparency & Accountability

## Based On
B Corp Governance criteria, F-A-T-H-E-R Leadership (Fairness, Accountability, Trust, Honesty, Equality, Respect), Markkula Center Publicity Test, Transparency International standards.

## Core Question

> Would you be comfortable if every aspect of this decision — your reasoning, the trade-offs, the outcomes — were made public?

## Evaluation Criteria

### Criteria 1: The Publicity Test
- **0–25**: Would be embarrassed or harmed if this decision was published
- **26–50**: Would prefer it not be public, but could defend it
- **51–75**: Would be fine with it being public
- **76–100**: Would actively welcome public scrutiny as a validation of integrity

### Criteria 2: Traceability
- **0–25**: No record of decision-making process exists
- **26–50**: Ad hoc or incomplete documentation
- **51–75**: Clear, documented decision trail
- **76–100**: Full audit trail with reasoning, alternatives considered, and trade-offs

### Criteria 3: Accountability Assignment
- **0–25**: No one is accountable for the decision or its outcomes
- **26–50**: Accountability unclear or diffuse
- **51–75**: Clear accountability assigned
- **76–100**: Named accountability with mechanisms for redress if harm occurs

### Criteria 4: Alignment with Stated Values
- **0–25**: Decision contradicts stated values without acknowledgment
- **26–50**: Inconsistency between values and action
- **51–75**: Generally aligned with stated values
- **76–100**: Exemplary — action actively demonstrates values in practice

### Criteria 5: Conflict of Interest Check
- **0–25**: Undisclosed conflict of interest present
- **26–50**: Potential conflict not fully examined
- **51–75**: Conflicts declared and managed
- **76–100**: No conflicts; or conflicts declared, managed, and mitigated

## Questions to Ask

- Would the business publish this decision publicly?
- Could the owner defend this in front of their customers, employees, or the public?
- Is the reasoning traceable and auditable?
- Does it align with the business's stated values and mission?
- Is there any conflict of interest?
- Would this stand up to scrutiny from a journalist, regulator, or ethical board?
- If someone was harmed by this decision, would the process be defensible?
- Is the language used to describe this decision honest and free of spin?

## Edge Cases

- **Privacy vs. transparency**: Personal privacy is legitimate. Transparency doesn't mean publishing everything — it means the *decision process* is transparent, not necessarily all data.
- **Strategic confidentiality**: Trade secrets and competitive confidentiality are valid ONLY if they don't conceal ethical violations. Test: "Is this confidential to protect a legitimate business interest, or to hide something unethical?"
- **Transparency theatre**: Publishing information nobody can understand or act on isn't transparency. A transparent decision is also an *understandable* one.
- **Chain of responsibility**: If a decision passes through multiple hands, accountability can dissolve. Check if each handoff has clear ownership.

## Flag Thresholds

- 🟢 Score ≥ 70: Proceed
- 🟡 Score 40–69: Flag — transparency gaps exist
- 🔴 Score < 40: Block — significant transparency or integrity concerns
